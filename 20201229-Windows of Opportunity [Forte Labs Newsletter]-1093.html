Dear humans,

As we wrap up the last few days of the year, I've been thinking a lot about goals.

Goals are the bread and butter of the productivity world. They are most people's first introduction to the idea that you can intentionally improve your effectiveness at work and in life.

Goals have become such a foundational part of how we think that they've faded into the background of our culture. We set goals when we start a new job, when we do financial planning, and even when we start dating.

In almost any endeavor we take on, we tend to be very comfortable with the idea that setting goals (or objectives, or targets, or milestones, or whatever else you call them) is a critical step in getting what we want.

But there is a flipside to that level of comfort: the way we think about goals goes largely unquestioned. There hasn't been much creativity or innovation going into understanding the true nature of goals, how best to formulate them, and how goal-setting might be evolving over time.

Companies and startups have made dramatic advancements in recent years in how they identify "objectives and key results." But on the level of individuals, we're stuck with frameworks that are decades old.

I realized recently that a concept I was introduced to last year has become very influential in how I think about personal goal-setting. It is called "Windows of Opportunity."

I've come to believe that Windows of Opportunity (WOOs) represent the most important leap forward in our understanding of goals since 1981, when the SMART goals framework was introduced.

WOOs recognize that there is now one factor that is more decisive than any other when it comes to reaching your ambitions: timing.

We have so many massive forces ‚Äì economic, political, societal, and historical ‚Äì shifting beneath us all the time, our success depends more than ever on our ability to ride those waves of change and harness their power in our favor.
